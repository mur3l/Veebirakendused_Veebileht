Cycle Lane

23 VEEBRUAR 2025

  1. Estonia

    2. Cyclists in Estonia enjoy specific rights and responsibilities under the Traffic Act.

    Cyclists must adhere to the same traffic rules as drivers of other vehicles when riding on a carriageway.

    Children aged 10-15 must have a cyclist's permit to ride on carriageways, and cyclists under 16 must wear a helmet.

    Cyclists can ride on cycle tracks, cycle lanes, or as close to the far right side of the carriageway as possible, except when making a left turn.

    Cyclists must not operate a vehicle while intoxicated, be distracted by other activities, or carry objects that hamper their driving.

  2. Finland

    3. Cyclists in Finland are required to follow specific traffic rules to ensure safety and compliance.

    Cyclists must use designated cycle paths if available, and if not, they should ride on the right side of the road or on the verge.

    Cyclists must obey traffic signals, give way to vehicles and pedestrians when required, and use appropriate lighting during dark conditions.

    Children under 12 may ride on pavements, but they must yield to pedestrians.

    Additionally, cyclists must give way to buses leaving bus stops if the cycle lane passes on the right side of the bus stop

  3. Europe

    4. Cyclists in Europe enjoy specific rights and responsibilities under various national and EU-wide regulations.

    According to the Vienna Convention, a cycle is defined as a vehicle with at least two wheels propelled solely by the muscular energy of the rider.

    Cyclists must ensure their bicycles are equipped with efficient brakes, a bell, and appropriate lighting and reflectors for visibility.

    In many European countries, the use of cycle helmets is mandatory for certain age groups or in specific situations.

    For example, helmets are required for all cyclists in Malta, for children up to 15 years in Sweden, and for cyclists outside urban areas in Spain.

    Cyclists must adhere to traffic rules, such as using designated cycle paths where available and giving way to pedestrians and other vehicles when required.

    In some countries, the use of cycle lanes marked with blue circle signs is compulsory, while blue square signs indicate optional cycleways.

    Cyclists should also be aware of local regulations regarding lighting, reflectors, and other safety equipment to ensure compliance and safety on the roads.
